# Co-Parenting Coach — Knowledge Base

This is the coach's working knowledge: the core ideas from modern parenting psychology,
**rewritten in our own words** as practical guidance. It draws on the bodies of work behind
*How to Talk So Kids Will Listen & Listen So Kids Will Talk* (Faber & Mazlish), *The
Whole-Brain Child* (Siegel & Bryson), and attachment theory (Bowlby, Ainsworth, and later
researchers). No copyrighted text is reproduced — these are paraphrased principles the coach
applies to the family's specific situation.

The coach's job is not to lecture these frameworks. It listens, reflects, and offers one or
two concrete moves the parent can try in the next five minutes.

---

## 0. How to coach (stance)

- **Connect first, advise second.** Acknowledge the parent's stress before problem-solving.
  A parent who feels judged can't hear advice.
- **Be concrete and small.** One or two specific things to try, not a curriculum. "Try
  naming what she's feeling out loud" beats "use emotional validation."
- **Ask before assuming.** A short clarifying question ("How old is he, and what happened
  right before?") makes the advice land.
- **Both parents on the same team.** This is a shared coach. Frame guidance as something the
  household does together, and help the two of them get aligned rather than taking sides.
- **Stay in your lane.** Coaching, not diagnosis or therapy. Escalate real risk (see the
  safety rules in the system prompt), and defer to pediatricians/therapists for clinical
  concerns.

## 1. Connection before correction (attachment)

Children behave best when they feel securely connected. Misbehavior is often a bid for
connection, a sign of an unmet need (tired, hungry, overwhelmed), or a skill they don't have
yet — not defiance for its own sake.

- A child who is melting down is **not giving you a hard time; they are having a hard time.**
- The relationship is the lever. Time spent connecting (eye contact, play, undivided
  attention) is not separate from discipline — it's what makes discipline work.
- "Filling the cup": brief, regular one-on-one attention reduces attention-seeking
  misbehavior later. Even 10 focused minutes counts.
- Repair matters more than perfection. After a rupture (you yelled, they melted down),
  reconnecting — "That was hard. I love you. Let's start over" — rebuilds security.

**Coach moves:** Look for the need under the behavior. Suggest a connection move before a
correction move.

---

## 2. Acknowledge feelings (the foundation skill)

Kids cooperate more when their feelings are accepted, even when their behavior is limited.
Denying or arguing with a feeling ("You're not really tired," "There's nothing to be scared
of") escalates it. Naming it calms it.

Four ways to acknowledge a feeling:
1. **Listen quietly and fully.** Sometimes attention is the whole intervention.
2. **Name the feeling.** "You sound really frustrated that the tower fell."
3. **Give the wish in fantasy.** "I bet you wish you could have ice cream for dinner every
   night." (Naming the wish ≠ granting it.)
4. **Accept the feeling while limiting the action.** "You're furious at your brother — I
   won't let you hit. Tell him with words how angry you are."

**Key distinction:** *all feelings are allowed; not all actions are.* This sentence does a
lot of work and the coach can offer it directly.

## 3. Whole-Brain strategies (how the brain works under stress)

A simple, useful model the coach can share with parents:

- **Upstairs vs. downstairs brain.** The "downstairs" brain (big feelings, fight/flight) is
  fully online from birth; the "upstairs" brain (reasoning, impulse control, empathy) is
  still under construction through the mid-20s. In a meltdown, the upstairs brain is offline
  — so reasoning, lecturing, and consequences won't reach it *yet*.
- **Name it to tame it.** Helping a child put words to a big feeling engages the upstairs
  brain and calms the downstairs storm. Narrate the feeling and the story: "You were so
  excited, and then it was time to stop, and that felt awful."
- **Connect and redirect.** First soothe the emotion (connect: tone, touch, validation),
  *then* address the behavior or teach the lesson (redirect). Don't try to teach during the
  storm.
- **Engage, don't enrage.** Appeal to the thinking brain rather than triggering the
  reactive one. A choice or a curious question engages; a command or threat enrages.
- **Move it or lose it.** Physical movement can shift a stuck emotional state — for some kids
  a quick physical reset (jumping, a walk, a silly dance) discharges the tension.

**Coach moves:** In the heat of the moment, the goal is regulation, not a lesson. Teach
after everyone is calm.

## 4. Engaging cooperation (instead of nagging/threatening)

Commands, blame, and threats invite resistance. These tools invite cooperation by treating
the child as capable:

- **Describe what you see / the problem.** "I see wet towels on the bed." (vs. "Why are you
  so careless?")
- **Give information.** "Towels dry out when they're hung up." Kids fill in the action.
- **Offer a choice.** "Bath now or after one more book?" Two acceptable options preserve
  autonomy.
- **Say it in a word (or a gesture).** "Teeth." Short, non-nagging, no lecture attached.
- **Describe what you feel.** "I get frustrated when I have to ask three times." (Describe
  your feeling, not their character.)
- **Write a note.** A little sign on the screen or the door can land where talking doesn't.
- **Make it playful.** Turn the task into a game, a race, or a silly voice — especially for
  younger kids, play breaks standoffs faster than pressure.

**Coach moves:** When a parent is stuck in a nag-loop, hand them one of these and have them
swap it in for the command they keep repeating.

## 5. Discipline as teaching, not punishment

"Discipline" means *to teach*. The aim is to build skills and judgment, not to make a child
suffer for a mistake. Punishment often teaches kids to avoid getting caught and damages
connection; it rarely teaches the missing skill.

Alternatives to punishment:
- **Natural / logical consequences.** Let the reasonable result happen ("If you throw the
  toy, the toy goes away for now"), connected and proportionate — not arbitrary or punitive.
- **Problem-solve together.** Once calm: name both sides' feelings, state the problem, and
  invite the child to brainstorm solutions with you. Kids uphold plans they helped make.
- **Express strong disapproval without attacking character.** "I am really upset that the
  wall got drawn on" — not "You're a bad kid."
- **Show how to make amends / repair.** Help them fix it (clean up, apologize, redo) rather
  than just punishing.
- **Set the limit, hold it kindly.** Empathy and firmness coexist: "You really want to keep
  playing. It's time to stop now. I'll help you."

**Coach moves:** Reframe "What punishment fits this?" into "What skill is missing, and how do
we teach it?"

## 6. Co-parenting alignment (the two-grown-ups layer)

This coach serves a household, so a big part of the job is helping two parents work as a team.

- **Disagree away from the kids.** Present a united front in the moment; hash out differences
  privately. Kids exploit (and are unsettled by) visible splits.
- **Aim for "good enough" consistency, not identical parenting.** Kids adapt to two styles
  fine if the core limits and values are shared. Agree on the few non-negotiables; relax the
  rest.
- **Back each other up, then debrief.** Support the parent who's in it, even if you'd have
  done it differently, and talk it through later.
- **Divide and recover.** Tag in when one parent is fried. Co-regulation applies to adults
  too — a regulated parent can lend calm to a dysregulated one.
- **Name your own triggers.** Help each parent notice which behaviors hook them personally
  (often echoes of their own childhood) so they can respond instead of react.

**Coach moves:** When the two parents disagree, surface the shared value underneath, and help
them pick one aligned approach to try this week.

## 7. Regulate yourself first (co-regulation)

A child borrows the parent's nervous system to calm down. You cannot pour calm from an empty,
activated state — so the first move in any meltdown is often the parent's own breath.

- Lower your voice and slow down; the child's system mirrors yours.
- It's okay to take a beat: "I need a second to calm down so I can help you."
- Self-compassion for the parent matters — guilt and shame make reactivity worse, not better.

**Coach moves:** If the parent is activated, coach *their* regulation first; the child's
behavior often settles once the adult does.

## 8. Developmental & temperament lens

Tailor every suggestion to the child's age and temperament — the same behavior means
different things at 2, 6, and 12.

- **Toddlers (1–3):** Big feelings, tiny impulse control. Tantrums are normal brain
  development, not manipulation. Keep language short; offer simple choices; expect to
  co-regulate a lot.
- **Preschool (3–5):** Magical thinking, testing limits, learning fairness. Great age for
  feeling-words, play-based cooperation, and simple problem-solving.
- **School-age (6–12):** Logic and fairness come online; they can genuinely problem-solve
  with you. Watch for shame — they're sensitive to feeling "bad."
- **Teens:** Pushing for autonomy is the developmental job, not a betrayal. Connection +
  respect for their growing independence beats control. Pick your battles.
- **Temperament:** Some kids are slow-to-warm, intense, highly sensitive, or very active.
  Match expectations to the actual child, not an ideal one. An intense kid isn't "too much" —
  they need more co-regulation and more runway for transitions.

**Coach moves:** Always anchor advice to *this* child's age and temperament; ask if unknown.

## 9. De-escalating in the moment (quick reference)

When a parent calls mid-storm, the coach prioritizes calm over teaching:

1. **You first.** One slow breath; drop your voice.
2. **Connect.** Get low, soften your face, offer presence over words: "I'm here."
3. **Name it.** "You're so upset right now." Don't reason or negotiate yet.
4. **Hold the limit kindly if needed.** "I won't let you hit. I'll hold your hands."
5. **Wait out the wave.** Big feelings crest and fall; let it pass before talking.
6. **Reconnect, then (much later) teach or problem-solve.**

The single most common correction the coach makes: parents try to **teach during the storm**.
Coach them to regulate and connect now, and save the lesson for after everyone is calm.
